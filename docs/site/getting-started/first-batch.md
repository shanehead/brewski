# Your first batch

A batch is what happens when you actually brew. Where a recipe is a plan, a batch is the record of what you did: the real volumes, the real gravity numbers, and everything that happened between kettle and glass.

## Starting a batch

Open the recipe you want to brew and hit **New Batch**. Brewski links the batch to the current version of that recipe. If you change the recipe later, your batch still shows exactly what you brewed.

## Brew day

On brew day, open the **Brew Day** tab. Log your pre-boil volume and gravity, then your post-boil numbers once the boil is done. These aren't required, but they're the best way to track your efficiency over time. A few batches in, you'll start to see patterns in your process.

## Fermentation and gravity readings

Once the wort is chilled and pitched, switch to the **Gravity** tab. Add a reading whenever you take one. Set the date, enter your gravity (Brewski accepts SG, Plato, or Brix depending on your settings), and optionally log the temperature. Plotting readings over time gives you a clear picture of fermentation progress and tells you when it's actually done rather than when you're just hoping it is.

## Packaging

When fermentation is finished, open the **Carbonation** tab. Use the priming sugar calculator to dial in your bottling additions, or use the CO2 pressure calculator if you're kegging. Enter your target volumes of CO2 and your current beer temperature, and Brewski does the rest.

## Finishing the batch

Once the beer is packaged and conditioned, mark the batch status as complete. Add tasting notes while the beer is fresh in your mind, and give it a rating. Over time, your batch history becomes a reference for what's working and what isn't.

## The link between batches and recipes

The batch always stays linked to the recipe version you brewed from. Update the recipe next time around and your old batch records are untouched. You'll always know exactly what went into each beer you made.
