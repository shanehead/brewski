# Your first recipe

A recipe in Brewski has four main pieces: ingredients (fermentables, hops, yeast), a mash, an equipment profile, and a stats sidebar that shows OG, FG, ABV, IBU, and SRM. That sidebar updates in real time as you add ingredients, so you always know where you stand.

Here's how to put your first recipe together.

## Step 1: Create a new recipe

Hit **New Recipe** in the left sidebar. Give it a name, something descriptive enough that you'll recognize it later. If you're targeting a specific beer style, pick one from the style dropdown. This isn't required, but it adds a target range to your stats so you can see how close you are.

## Step 2: Add your fermentables

Open the **Fermentables** tab. Hit **Add Fermentable** and search for your base malt. "Pale Malt 2-Row" is a common starting point. Set the amount, and Brewski will calculate how it affects your OG and color.

Repeat for any specialty malts. Caramel 60, Munich, Roasted Barley — add them the same way. Each one updates the stats sidebar as you go.

## Step 3: Add hops

Open the **Hops** tab and hit **Add Hop**. Search for your variety, set the alpha acid percentage, choose how you're using it (Boil, Whirlpool, or Dry Hop), and set the time. Boil additions drive most of your bitterness. Whirlpool and dry hop additions contribute more to aroma than IBU.

Add as many hop additions as your recipe needs.

## Step 4: Add yeast

Open the **Yeast** tab and add your yeast. Set the attenuation percentage if you know it. Brewski uses this to calculate your estimated FG and ABV, so it's worth getting right. Your yeast packet or the manufacturer's website will have the number.

## Step 5: Set up your mash

Open the **Mash** tab. Brewski starts you with a default single-infusion step already in place. Adjust the temperature and water-to-grain ratio if you need to. For most ales, a 152–154°F (67–68°C) mash works well.

## Step 6: Save

Watch the stats sidebar as you work. When you're happy with where things land, hit **Save**.

::: tip You don't have to fill everything in at once
Save early and come back. A recipe with just a grain bill and a couple of hop additions is a perfectly valid starting point. Add the rest when you're ready.
:::
