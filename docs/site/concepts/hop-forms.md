# Hop forms & usage types

Hops come in several physical forms, and which one you use affects how you calculate bitterness and how you handle them on brew day. Brewski adjusts the IBU math based on the hop form you select.

## Pellets (T-90)

**Pellet hops** (T-90) are the default for most homebrewers. They're made by grinding whole hops and extruding them into small pellets, which stabilizes them and extends shelf life significantly. Pellets dissolve into the wort during the boil and are easy to measure and store.

Pellets are Brewski's baseline for IBU calculations. When you add a hop addition without specifying form, pellet utilization is assumed.

## Whole/leaf hops

**Whole hops** (also called leaf hops) are the dried hop cones, minimally processed. Some brewers prefer them for dry hopping or for specific styles where they prefer the character. They have a few tradeoffs: utilization in the boil is roughly 10% lower than pellets because the lupulin glands are less exposed, and they absorb more wort volume (usually 0.15-0.25 liters per 100g, depending on how much they absorb).

If you're using whole hops in the boil, you'll need slightly more weight to hit the same IBU target as pellets.

## Cryo hops

**Cryo hops** are concentrated lupulin powder, made by separating and freeze-concentrating the lupulin glands from whole hops. The result has roughly twice the alpha acid and oil content of standard T-90 pellets. That means you use roughly half the weight to achieve the same IBU contribution.

Set the hop form to **Cryo** in Brewski and the IBU calculation adjusts automatically. Cryo hops are popular for dry hopping and late additions because the high oil concentration delivers intense aroma for a given weight, and the lower vegetative matter reduces grassy/leafy character.

## T-45 hops

**T-45 hops** fall between T-90 and cryo in concentration. They're less common than the other forms and produced by a different milling process that retains more lupulin relative to vegetative material than standard pellets. Less widely available than T-90 or cryo.

## Usage types

The form of your hop determines how you handle it, but the usage type determines what it contributes to the beer:

**Boil:** The workhorse bittering addition. Alpha acids isomerize at boil temperatures, producing bitterness. The longer the boil addition, the more bitterness. Flavor and aroma compounds mostly boil off during a long addition, so 60-minute boil hops are primarily bitterness, while 10-15 minute additions start adding some flavor too.

**Whirlpool:** Added after the heat is off while the wort circulates. The wort is still hot enough to extract compounds, but at a lower temperature that preserves more aroma. You get more flavor and aroma than a boil addition at comparable weight, with less bitterness.

**Hopstand:** A deliberate hot steep after flameout, typically at a specific temperature (often 80-95°C). Temperature determines the character: hotter hopstands contribute more IBUs, cooler hopstands shift toward aroma and flavor with minimal bitterness.

**Dry hop:** Goes into the fermenter during fermentation or conditioning, at room temperature or colder. Contributes zero bitterness. This is where you get the freshest, most aromatic hop character in the finished beer. Different varieties shine in the dry hop that might not stand out in the boil.
