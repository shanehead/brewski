# Yeast pitch rate & starters

How much yeast you add to your wort matters more than most homebrewers realize. Too little and the yeast are stressed, leading to off-flavors, slow starts, stuck fermentations, and potentially ruined batches. Too much and you can get flat, yeasty-tasting beer with poor head retention. Getting the pitch rate right is one of the highest-leverage things you can do for fermentation quality.

## What pitch rate means

**Pitch rate** is expressed in millions of cells per milliliter of wort per degree Plato (M/mL/°P). That sounds complicated, but it's just a way of accounting for both the volume of wort and its gravity: a bigger, stronger beer needs more cells than a small, light one.

The standard targets are:

- **Ales:** 0.75 M/mL/°P
- **Lagers:** 1.5 M/mL/°P

Lagers need double the cells because cold fermentation is stressful for yeast. More cells means each individual cell has to work less hard, reducing stress byproducts and giving you the clean profile lagers require.

A typical homebrewing batch: 20 liters of wort at OG 1.052 (13°P). For an ale that's `0.75 × 20,000 × 13 = 195 billion cells`. For a lager it's `1.5 × 20,000 × 13 = 390 billion cells`.

## Liquid yeast viability

Liquid yeast doesn't last forever. Cells die off after packaging at a predictable rate: typically around 20% per month of storage, though that varies by manufacturer and storage conditions. A fresh vial or smack pack ships with roughly 100 billion cells. Two months later, 65-80 billion might be viable. At four months, you're looking at significantly less.

This matters because you're working with a fraction of what the package originally contained. That's why starters exist.

## Yeast starters

A **starter** grows more yeast cells before brew day by giving the yeast a small wort to ferment. The idea is simple: make 1-2 liters of 1.030-1.040 wort (usually from dry malt extract), pitch your yeast package into it, let it ferment for 12-24 hours, then pitch the whole thing into your main batch.

A **stir plate** dramatically speeds up cell growth by keeping the yeast in suspension and oxygenating continuously. With a stir plate and 24 hours, you can roughly double the starting cell count from a single packet. Without a stir plate, you still get growth, just less of it.

Brewski's [Pitch Rate calculator](/reference/calculators/pitch-rate) tells you how many cells you need, estimates current viability from the manufacture date, and calculates whether a starter is needed and what size.

## Dry yeast

Dry yeast plays by different rules. Most dry yeast packets contain 200 billion cells or more at viability rates well above 90%, because the drying process selects for robust, shelf-stable cells. For a standard homebrew batch (18-25 liters, OG under 1.065), a single 11g packet is usually enough without a starter.

For very high-gravity beers or lagers, consider two packets or a small starter even with dry yeast. The math works the same way, the starting cell count is just much higher.

## Signs of underpitching

If fermentation doesn't start within 24-48 hours, or produces a lot of sulfur, harsh fusel alcohols (hot, solvent-like flavor), or excessive esters (banana, pear), underpitching is a likely cause. A fresh, properly-sized pitch usually kicks off within 6-12 hours and produces clean, predictable results.
