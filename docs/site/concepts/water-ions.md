# Water ions & their effects

Water is never just H2O. It contains dissolved minerals, and those minerals affect your beer in real, measurable ways. Six ions matter most to brewing. Understanding what each one does lets you dial in your water for a specific style or flavor profile.

## The six key ions

| Ion | Effect | Target range |
|---|---|---|
| **Calcium (Ca)** | Promotes enzyme activity, yeast health, and clarity. Lowers mash pH. The most important ion to get right. | 50-150 ppm |
| **Magnesium (Mg)** | A yeast nutrient at low levels. Turns harsh and even laxative above 30 ppm. Most water already has some. | Under 30 ppm |
| **Sodium (Na)** | Adds roundness and fullness at low levels. Tastes salty and harsh above 150 ppm. | Under 150 ppm |
| **Chloride (Cl)** | Accentuates malt character, body, and sweetness. A higher Cl:SO4 ratio pushes the beer toward malty. | Style-dependent |
| **Sulfate (SO4)** | Accentuates hop dryness and bitterness. A higher SO4:Cl ratio makes hops taste crisper and drier. | Style-dependent |
| **Bicarbonate (HCO3)** | Raises mash pH. Useful when brewing with dark malts that would otherwise drop pH too low. Keep it low for pale styles. | Style-dependent |

## The Cl:SO4 ratio

The single most useful tool for shaping beer character is the **chloride-to-sulfate ratio**. Neither ion by itself determines flavor, but their balance does.

Lean toward chloride for malt-forward beers: stouts, porters, English ales, Marzen, dunkel. Lean toward sulfate for hop-forward beers: West Coast IPA, session IPA, English bitter. Most balanced styles land somewhere in between.

A rough guide:
- Chloride dominant (2:1 or more): soft, round, malt-forward
- Roughly equal: balanced
- Sulfate dominant (2:1 or more): dry, crisp, hop-forward

Brewing water profiles like Burton-on-Trent (very high sulfate) helped define the dry, bitter character of English pale ales. Munich water (higher bicarbonate and chloride) suited the soft, malt-forward lagers that originated there.

## Adjusting your water

You adjust ion levels by adding minerals to your brewing water. Common additions:

- **Gypsum (CaSO4)**: adds calcium and sulfate
- **Calcium chloride (CaCl2)**: adds calcium and chloride
- **Epsom salt (MgSO4)**: adds magnesium and sulfate
- **Table salt (NaCl)**: adds sodium and chloride
- **Baking soda (NaHCO3)**: raises pH and adds sodium and bicarbonate
- **Chalk (CaCO3)**: adds calcium and bicarbonate; dissolves poorly without CO2

Brewski's Water tab calculates your ion levels in real time as you add mineral additions, so you can see exactly where you land before you brew.

## Starting water matters

If your tap water is already high in sulfate or bicarbonate, you may need to dilute it with RO (reverse osmosis) or distilled water before building your profile. Starting from RO water gives you a blank slate to build from. Starting from your tap gives you a known baseline to adjust from. Either approach works; what matters is knowing what you're starting with.
