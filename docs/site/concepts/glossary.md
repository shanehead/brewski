# Glossary A-Z

Quick definitions for terms you'll encounter in Brewski and in brewing generally.

---

## A

**ABV (Alcohol By Volume):** The percentage of your beer that's alcohol. Calculated from the difference between OG and FG using `(OG - FG) × 131.25`. A standard American pale ale is around 5% ABV; a session beer is typically under 4.5%.

**Adjunct:** Any fermentable ingredient that isn't malted barley. Corn, rice, oats, wheat, honey, fruit, and sugar are all adjuncts. Some adjuncts lighten body (rice, corn), some add fermentables without flavor (sugar), and some add specific character (oats for mouthfeel, honey for dryness).

**Alpha acids:** The compounds in hops that become bitterness during the boil. They're not bitter themselves until heat converts them into iso-alpha acids through isomerization. Alpha acid percentage is the primary factor driving a hop's IBU contribution.

**Apparent attenuation:** The percentage of original fermentable extract that the yeast converted, calculated as `(OG - FG) / (OG - 1.000) × 100`. It's "apparent" because alcohol's low density makes the FG reading look lower than the actual remaining sugar content. See also: Real attenuation.

**Attenuation:** How completely yeast fermented the available sugars. High attenuation means a drier beer. Low attenuation means more residual sweetness and body. Yeast strains have typical attenuation ranges, and mash temperature also plays a large role.

---

## B

**BeerXML:** An open XML file format for sharing beer recipes between brewing software. Brewski can import recipes from Brewfather, BeerSmith, and other BeerXML-compatible tools, and can export your own recipes in the same format.

**Beta acids:** A different group of hop acids that contribute some bitterness but don't isomerize efficiently in the boil. They matter more in dry hopping and conditioning, where oxidation slowly converts them into bitter compounds over time. Not as well understood or as directly tracked as alpha acids.

**BIAB (Brew In A Bag):** A simplified all-grain brewing method where you mash grain in a large mesh bag inside the brew kettle, then lift the bag out to drain. No separate mash tun needed. BIAB typically has slightly lower efficiency than traditional three-vessel systems, but the simplicity makes it very popular with homebrewers.

**BU:GU ratio:** A balance metric that divides IBU by gravity units (OG - 1, times 1000). Around 1.0 is balanced. Lower is more malt-forward, higher is more bitter. Useful for comparing bitterness balance between recipes at different strengths.

---

## C

**Carbonation:** CO2 dissolved in beer, measured in volumes of CO2 (how many times the liquid volume the dissolved gas would occupy at atmospheric pressure). Most ales are 2.0-2.5 volumes. Hefeweizens and Belgian ales often hit 3.0-4.0. Carbonation affects perceived body, aroma, and mouthfeel significantly.

**Cold crash:** Chilling your fermenter to near-freezing (0-2°C / 32-36°F) for 24-72 hours before packaging. Yeast, hops particles, and other solids settle out quickly, giving you clearer beer without filtration. Most homebrewers cold crash in a fridge or chest freezer.

**Conditioning:** The period after primary fermentation where beer matures, clarifies, and carbonates. This happens in a secondary vessel, a keg, or in the bottle (through bottle conditioning). Conditioning time varies by style: a lager might condition for weeks, a session ale might be ready in days.

---

## D

**Dry hop:** Adding hops to the fermenter during or after fermentation, at room temperature or colder. Dry hopping contributes zero bitterness and lots of aroma, giving you the freshest, most pungent hop character possible. Time, temperature, and variety all affect the result.

---

## E

**EBC (European Brewery Convention):** The European scale for measuring beer color, related to SRM by `EBC = SRM × 1.97`. A pale lager might be 5-8 EBC. A stout might be 70-100 EBC. Used on most European malt data sheets.

**Efficiency (mash):** See Mash efficiency.

---

## F

**FG (Final Gravity):** The specific gravity of your beer after fermentation is complete. Lower FG means the yeast ate more sugar, producing drier beer with more alcohol. Higher FG means more residual sweetness. Typical finished beers fall between 1.006 and 1.020 depending on style, yeast, and mash temperature.

**Flocculence:** A yeast strain's tendency to clump together and drop to the bottom of the fermenter. High-flocculating strains settle out quickly and produce clearer beer but may stop fermenting before they've eaten all the sugar. Low-flocculating strains stay in suspension longer, ferment more completely, but take longer to clear.

**Flameout:** The moment you turn off the heat at the end of the boil. Hops added at flameout go in at this point, while the wort is still very hot but no longer boiling vigorously. They contribute flavor and aroma, and some bitterness depending on how long the wort stays hot afterward.

---

## G

**Grain bill:** The full list of malts and other fermentable grains in a recipe: base malts, specialty malts, adjunct grains. The grain bill determines a beer's color, body, fermentability, and base flavor before hops or yeast come into play.

**Gravity:** A measure of the sugar concentration in wort or beer, expressed as specific gravity (SG), Plato, or Brix. Higher gravity means more dissolved sugar, more potential alcohol, and more food for yeast. See [Understanding gravity](/concepts/gravity).

---

## H

**HLT (Hot Liquor Tank):** The vessel used to heat and hold water before and during brewing. "Hot liquor" is just brewer-speak for hot water. In a three-vessel system (HLT, mash tun, kettle), the HLT provides strike water for the mash and sparge water.

**Hopstand:** A hot steep of hops after flameout, at a specific temperature. Isomerization continues down to about 80°C (176°F), so hopstand temperature determines how much bitterness vs. aroma the addition contributes. Higher temperature gives more IBUs, lower temperature gives more aroma.

**IBU (International Bitterness Units):** The concentration of iso-alpha acids in beer, in mg/L. IBUs quantify bitterness potential. Perceived bitterness also depends on the balance with malt sweetness, carbonation, and other factors. See [IBU: bitterness explained](/concepts/ibu).

**Infusion mash:** The most common mashing method: you add all the strike water at once and hold the mash at a single temperature until conversion is complete. Simple and reliable for most styles. Contrast with step mashing, where you raise the temperature in stages to activate different enzymes.

---

## K

**Krausen:** The thick, foamy head that forms on top of actively fermenting wort. It's yeast and CO2 and proteins, and it's a sign that fermentation is going strong. Krausen can rise dramatically in the first 24-72 hours, which is why you need headspace in your fermenter. It falls back down as fermentation slows.

---

## L

**Lovibond:** The unit used to measure the color of individual malts and hops. Pale malt is 1-3°L. Crystal 60 is 60°L. Chocolate malt is around 350°L. Lovibond values from each fermentable feed into Brewski's color calculation using the Morey formula to estimate final beer color in SRM.

---

## M

**Mash:** The process of soaking crushed grain in hot water to convert starches into fermentable sugars via enzymatic activity. The mash produces wort, which is then boiled, hopped, chilled, and fermented. Mash temperature and duration are the primary variables you control.

**Mash efficiency:** The percentage of the theoretical maximum sugar content you actually extract from your grain during the mash and sparge. Typical homebrew efficiency is 65-80%. Higher efficiency means more fermentable extract per kg of grain, which affects how much grain you need to hit a target OG.

**Mash pH:** The pH of your mash liquid during the mash. The optimal range for enzyme activity is 5.2-5.4. Too high (alkaline) reduces efficiency and extracts harsh tannins. Water chemistry adjustments and acidified malts are used to bring pH into range.

---

## O

**OG (Original Gravity):** The specific gravity of your wort measured before you pitch yeast. It's your baseline for everything that follows: ABV, attenuation, and fermentation progress. Even if you log nothing else, log your OG.

---

## P

**Packaging:** The step where you transfer finished beer into its final container: keg, bottles, or cans. Force carbonation (kegging) and bottle conditioning (adding priming sugar before sealing) are the two main methods homebrewers use.

**Pitch / Pitch rate:** Pitching is the act of adding yeast to your wort. Pitch rate is the amount of yeast you add, expressed in million cells per mL per degree Plato. The right pitch rate for your batch size and gravity gives yeast a healthy start and prevents fermentation stress.

**Plato:** A measurement of sugar concentration expressed as grams of sugar per 100g of liquid. Commercial brewers use Plato; homebrewers more commonly use SG. 12°P is approximately OG 1.048. Brewski converts between them automatically.

**Priming sugar:** A small, calculated amount of sugar added to beer just before bottling (or added to a keg for natural carbonation). Residual yeast consumes the sugar and produces CO2 in the sealed container. The amount determines the carbonation level. Brewski's carbonation calculator figures out the exact amount based on your target volumes of CO2 and batch size.

---

## R

**Real attenuation:** Attenuation calculated by correcting for alcohol's effect on density. Because alcohol is lighter than water, FG readings understate how much sugar remains. Real attenuation is always a few percentage points lower than apparent attenuation and represents the true fermentation extent. Mostly relevant in commercial quality control.

---

## S

**SG (Specific Gravity):** The ratio of a liquid's density to the density of water. Pure water is 1.000. Wort with dissolved sugars is denser, so OG readings like 1.050 or 1.080 mean the wort is 5% or 8% denser than water, respectively. SG is the most common gravity unit in homebrewing.

**Sparging:** Rinsing the grain bed after the mash to extract remaining sugars. You run hot water (around 75-77°C / 167-170°F) through the grain to rinse out sugars that didn't make it into the first runnings. Batch sparging (adding all the water at once) and fly sparging (continuous slow rinse) are the two main approaches.

**SRM (Standard Reference Method):** The US standard for measuring beer color. Higher SRM means darker beer. Pale lagers are 1-4 SRM, amber ales are 9-14 SRM, and stouts are 23-40 SRM. Brewski calculates SRM from your grain bill using the Morey formula.

**Starter (yeast):** A small pre-fermentation to grow more yeast cells before brew day. You make a small wort (1-2 liters at around 1.035 OG), pitch your yeast package, let it ferment for 12-24 hours, and then pitch the entire starter into your main batch. Starters are especially important for liquid yeast that's been stored for more than a few weeks.

---

## T

**Trub:** The thick sediment that collects at the bottom of the kettle after the boil. It's made up of coagulated proteins (hot break), hop material, and other solids. Most homebrewers leave trub behind in the kettle when transferring to the fermenter, though some argue a bit of trub improves yeast health.

---

## V

**Viability (yeast):** The percentage of yeast cells in a package that are still alive. Fresh liquid yeast from a manufacturer ships at close to 100% viability. Viability decreases over time, typically by around 20% per month. Brewski's pitch rate calculator estimates viability from the manufacture date to give you an accurate cell count.

**Volumes of CO2:** The unit used to express carbonation level. One volume means the dissolved CO2, if released, would occupy the same volume as the liquid. American lagers are typically 2.5 volumes. Belgian ales can reach 3.5-4.0 volumes. Temperature and pressure during carbonation determine how many volumes dissolve.

---

## W

**Wort:** Unfermented beer: the sugar-rich liquid that comes out of the mash and boil. It's what you pitch your yeast into. Before fermentation, it's wort. After fermentation, it's beer. Pronounced to rhyme with "hurt."

**Wort Correction Factor (WCF):** A correction applied to refractometer readings taken during or after fermentation. Alcohol throws off the refractometer's sugar-density reading, making FG readings inaccurate without correction. Brewski applies the Novotny formula automatically: `1.0 - 0.00085683 × correctedOG + 0.0034941 × correctedFG`. Always use a hydrometer for FG confirmation, or use Brewski's refractometer calculator.

---

## Y

**Yeast nutrient:** A blend of vitamins, minerals, and nitrogen compounds added to wort to support healthy yeast growth. More commonly used in wine and mead brewing, where the fermentable is nutritionally sparse. In beer, the malt usually provides adequate nutrition, but nutrient additions can help with high-adjunct or very high-gravity recipes.
