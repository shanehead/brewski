# Gravity Conversions

The **Gravity Conversions** calculator converts between the three common gravity scales used in brewing. Enter a value in any unit and get the equivalent in all three. You'll find it under **Tools** in the left sidebar.

## Input

Enter your gravity value in whichever unit your instrument reports. The calculator accepts SG, Plato, or Brix.

## Output

| Output | Description |
|---|---|
| **SG** | Specific Gravity |
| **Plato (°P)** | Degrees Plato |
| **Brix (°Bx)** | Degrees Brix |

## The three scales

All three scales measure the same thing: how much dissolved sugar is in your wort or beer. They're just different ways of expressing that concentration.

| Scale | How it works | Typical range |
|---|---|---|
| **SG** | Ratio of wort density to water density. Water = 1.000. | 1.030 (session) to 1.100+ (barleywine) |
| **Plato (°P)** | Grams of sugar per 100 grams of solution. SG 1.050 is approximately 12.4°P. | ~7.5°P to ~24°P |
| **Brix (°Bx)** | Used in winemaking and with refractometers. Numerically very close to Plato for wort. | Same range as Plato |

## Which scale should you use?

Use whichever unit your instrument reports, then convert from there.

Homebrewing hydrometers almost always read in SG. Refractometers read in Brix. Commercial breweries tend to prefer Plato. Malt spec sheets sometimes list color in Lovibond (see [Color Conversions](/reference/calculators/color-conversions)) and gravity in Plato.

## Tips

**Brix and Plato are very close but not identical.** For homebrewing purposes the difference is negligible. If you're writing software or need precision, use the specific conversion formula rather than assuming 1:1.

**SG is the most common unit in homebrew recipes.** If a recipe calls for an OG of 1.052, that's SG. Plugging that into this calculator will tell you it's about 12.9°P or 12.9°Bx.
