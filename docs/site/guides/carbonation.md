# Carbonation

Getting carbonation right matters. Too little and the beer is flat. Too much and you're cleaning up ceiling. Brewski's carbonation calculator lives in the **Overview** tab and gives you the numbers for both bottling and kegging.

## Unlock the carbonation section

The carbonation section appears when your batch status is **Conditioning** or **Packaged**. Open the **Overview** tab and set the status by clicking the appropriate stage in the status bar at the top.

Once active, you'll see the carbonation section at the bottom of the page.

## What you're looking at

The section shows:

- **Target CO2 volumes**: pulled from your recipe. This is read-only on the batch.
- **Packaging temperature**: you enter this.
- **Sugar type**: you pick this.
- **Batch size**: pulled from your measurements.
- **Priming sugar (grams)**: the amount to add for bottling.
- **Serving pressure (kPa)**: the pressure to set for kegging.

Both outputs are shown at the same time. Use whichever one applies to how you're packaging.

## Setting your packaging temperature

Enter the temperature of your beer at the **end of fermentation**, not after a cold crash. This is used to estimate how much CO2 is already dissolved in the beer, which affects how much priming sugar or pressure you need to reach your target.

Using your cold crash temperature here will give you a falsely low dissolved CO2 estimate, which means you'll add too much sugar or set pressure too high. Use the fermentation temperature.

## Choosing a sugar type

Pick one of the three options: **Corn Sugar**, **Table Sugar**, or **Dry Malt Extract**. All three will get you to the same carbonation level. The difference is the amount you need.

- **Corn Sugar (dextrose)** is the most common choice. It dissolves easily and ferments cleanly.
- **Table Sugar (sucrose)** works just as well. Slightly less by weight.
- **Dry Malt Extract (DME)** requires more grams for the same result, since it's not fully fermentable.

If you don't have a preference, go with Corn Sugar.

## CO2 volumes by style

Not sure what your target should be? Here are common ranges:

| Style | CO2 volumes |
|---|---|
| British ales | 1.8-2.2 |
| American ales | 2.3-2.6 |
| Lagers | 2.3-2.6 |
| Hefeweizens and Belgian styles | 3.0-3.5 |

These are starting points. Some styles have wide ranges, and personal preference plays a role too.

## Changing the CO2 target

The carbonation target (CO2 volumes) is set on the recipe, not the batch. If you need to adjust it, go to your recipe's **Overview** tab and update it there. The change will flow through to the batch carbonation section.

::: tip Bottling tip
Mix your priming sugar solution with a small amount of boiled water, let it cool, then add it to your bottling bucket before racking the beer on top. That way it gets distributed evenly without stirring in oxygen.
:::
