# Yeast

Yeast is what turns wort into beer. It determines how much of the fermentable sugar gets eaten, which sets your final gravity and your ABV. Pick the right strain and it also contributes its own flavors and aromas to the beer.

## Adding a yeast

Open the **Ingredients** tab and find the **Yeast** card. Hit **Add Yeast**.

Search by strain name or product ID. Select the strain and it's added with its typical attenuation value pre-filled.

## Attenuation %

Attenuation is how much of the fermentable sugar the yeast will consume. Higher attenuation means a drier beer and more alcohol. Lower attenuation leaves more residual sugar, which means more body and sweetness.

US-05, one of the most common dry ale yeasts, attenuates around 81%. A Belgian Trappist strain might hit 78–82% depending on the beer. An English ale strain might come in at 70–75%, leaving a rounder, fuller body.

This number directly drives the FG and ABV estimates in the stats sidebar. If you change the attenuation value, you'll see FG and ABV update immediately.

## Using multiple strains

If your recipe uses more than one yeast strain (a house blend or a mixed fermentation, for example), add them all. Brewski uses the first strain's attenuation to estimate FG. The others are recorded for your notes but don't affect the calculation.

## Lab and product ID

These fields are optional but worth filling in. When you're reordering a strain you loved or troubleshooting a fermentation, knowing exactly which product you pitched matters. It also helps when comparing batches later.

## Amount

Log how much yeast you're pitching. This field doesn't affect any calculations, but it's useful for your records. If you want to know how much you should be pitching for a given batch size and gravity, use the **Pitch Rate** calculator in Tools. It accounts for yeast type, cell counts, and whether you're using dry or liquid yeast.

## Dry yeast vs. liquid yeast

Dry yeast doesn't need a starter for most homebrew-scale batches. One or two packets is usually enough for a standard gravity ale. Liquid yeast often needs a starter, especially for big beers or lagers where you need a much higher cell count. The Pitch Rate calculator will tell you exactly where you stand.

::: tip Secondary fermentation
If you're adding yeast to secondary (for bottle conditioning or mixed fermentation), check the **Add to secondary** checkbox. It keeps your records clean and accurate.
:::
