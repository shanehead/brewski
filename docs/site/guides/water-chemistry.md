# Water chemistry

Water chemistry affects mash pH, flavor perception, and hop character. You don't have to use it, but it's worth learning once you're dialing in a style. The good news: Brewski makes it easy to experiment without needing a chemistry degree.

## Open the Water tab

Head to the **Water** tab on any recipe. You'll see two dropdowns at the top: **Mash Water** and **Sparge Water**. These let you pick a starting water profile from the library, things like Balanced, Soft, Burton-on-Trent, and others. You can also pick a profile you've set up yourself in the ingredient library.

The profile sets the base ion levels for your water source. From there, you can layer on mineral additions to nudge the numbers toward your target.

## Adding mineral additions

Below the water source dropdowns, you'll find fields for mineral additions, applied to your mash and sparge water separately. Common additions include gypsum (calcium sulfate), calcium chloride, baking soda, and others.

Enter the grams you plan to add. The calculated ion levels update immediately so you can see where you'll land.

## What to aim for

For most styles, 50 to 150 ppm calcium is a good starting range. It supports enzyme activity in the mash and generally improves beer clarity and flavor.

The sulfate-to-chloride balance is where the character of the water really shapes the beer:

- **High sulfate** (relative to chloride): drier, crisper, more bitter. Great for IPAs and pale ales.
- **High chloride** (relative to sulfate): fuller, rounder, maltier. Works well for stouts, porters, and malty lagers.

You don't need to nail a perfect ratio on your first brew. Even small shifts will have a noticeable effect.

## Start simple

If water chemistry is new to you, don't try to build a profile from scratch. Pick a water profile close to your actual tap water and make small adjustments. Brew the beer, taste it, and adjust from there.

See [Water ions & their effects](/concepts/water-ions) for a breakdown of what each ion does to your beer.
